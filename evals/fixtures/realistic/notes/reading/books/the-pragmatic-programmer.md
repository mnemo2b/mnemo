# The Pragmatic Programmer — Hunt & Thomas

## Core stance

- **Take responsibility** — own your code, your decisions, your career
- **Don't live with broken windows** — small neglects compound
- **Good enough software** — know when to stop polishing
- **Your knowledge portfolio** — invest in skills like investments, diversify

## Tips that stuck

- **DRY** — every piece of knowledge should have a single, unambiguous representation
- **Orthogonality** — changes in one area don't ripple through unrelated areas
- **Reversibility** — prefer decisions you can undo; flag the irreversible ones for careful thought
- **Tracer bullets** — end-to-end thin slice first, before building the perfect middle
- **Prototypes** — throw away
- **Domain languages** — make the code read like the problem
- **Estimating** — show the error bars; don't pretend you know

## Debugging

- "select" isn't broken. Check your code first.
- The bug is almost always in the code you wrote, not the library
- Rubber duck. Explaining out loud reveals the mismatch between model and reality.
- Fix the root cause, not the symptom. Fixing the symptom lets the bug return.

## Communicating

- Tests as documentation
- Plain-language specs over diagrams
- Know your audience — bosses want outcomes, peers want constraints, users want behavior
- Criticize ideas, not people

## The 20th anniversary edition

Updated examples (less "Java/C++", more language-agnostic), CI/CD, version control assumed, property-based testing added. Worth reading even if you read the original.

## Takeaway

The tips are the value, not the framing. Treat it as a reference, not a narrative. Re-read once a year; different tips hit at different points in your career.
