# Deep Work — Cal Newport

## Core claim

The ability to focus without distraction on cognitively demanding tasks is rare, valuable, and increasingly scarce. Those who cultivate it will thrive.

## Deep vs shallow

- **Deep work** — concentrated, novel, skill-building
- **Shallow work** — logistical, easily replicable, email, meetings

Most knowledge workers' calendars are 80%+ shallow. Move that number toward deep.

## Strategies

- **Monastic** — isolate entirely (Knuth, doesn't do email)
- **Bimodal** — alternating blocks of deep and shallow (days or weeks at a time)
- **Rhythmic** — scheduled daily deep work blocks (Seinfeld chains)
- **Journalistic** — fit deep work in whenever you can (expert mode)

Rhythmic is the pragmatic default for most.

## Tactics

- **Ritual** — same time, same place, same process
- **Grand gestures** — if it matters, go somewhere expensive/isolated
- **Don't work alone** — intellectual collaboration amplifies deep work
- **Execute like a business** — focus on a few goals, measure lead indicators, keep a scoreboard, regular review cadence

## Attention residue

Switching tasks leaves cognitive residue — performance on task B is worse because part of your brain is still on task A. Batch similar work. Avoid "just a quick check" of email mid-deep-block.

## Criticism

- Knowledge economy has roles (support, sales, management) where deep work isn't the job
- The book underweights how much ambient thinking happens during "shallow" tasks
- Some advice is impractical without significant autonomy

## Takeaways I apply

- 2-hour morning block, phone in another room, browser blocked
- Email/slack in batches at fixed times, not continuously
- "Time blocking" — every weekday hour has a purpose, reviewed at end of day
- No meetings before noon where possible
