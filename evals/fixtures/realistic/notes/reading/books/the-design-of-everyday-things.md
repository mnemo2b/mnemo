# The Design of Everyday Things — Don Norman

## Key ideas

- **Affordances** — what a thing lets you do, often signaled by its shape (handles for pulling, plates for pushing)
- **Signifiers** — visible cues that communicate affordances ("Push" sign on a door)
- **Mental models** — the user's theory of how something works; design to match or teach
- **Discoverability** — can users figure it out without a manual?
- **Feedback** — every action should produce an immediate, visible response

## Norman doors

The book's most famous concept: doors that don't tell you whether to push or pull, and you get it wrong. The failure is the door, not the user. Principle: when users fail, blame design.

## Seven stages of action

Goal → plan → specify → perform → perceive → interpret → compare. Designs fail at any step. Good design removes friction across all seven.

## Gulfs

- **Gulf of execution** — gap between what users want to do and what the system lets them do
- **Gulf of evaluation** — gap between system state and users' ability to read it

Bridge both sides and the experience clicks.

## Application

- For software: menu labels that say what they do, not jargon
- For websites: discoverable actions, no buried interactions
- For physical things: shape implies use
- Error recovery matters more than preventing errors (you can't prevent them all)

## Why it still matters

Every time a microwave panel confuses you, every time a new app buries a basic feature — this book explains the why. Ages remarkably well.
