# Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman

## Core model

- **System 1** — fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional
- **System 2** — slow, deliberate, effortful, rational

We like to think we operate in System 2. Mostly we're in System 1, rationalizing after the fact.

## Heuristics + biases

Some that stuck:

- **Availability heuristic** — we overweight recent / vivid examples (plane crashes > car crashes in our fear)
- **Anchoring** — first number seen skews estimates. Even irrelevant numbers.
- **Loss aversion** — losing $100 hurts 2-3x more than gaining $100 feels good
- **Endowment effect** — we value things more once we own them
- **Planning fallacy** — we're systematically optimistic about timelines
- **Sunk cost** — past investment shouldn't drive forward decisions, but we let it
- **Hindsight bias** — past events feel predictable in retrospect

## Prospect theory

People evaluate gains and losses relative to a reference point, not absolute wealth. Losses loom larger. Risk preferences flip: we're risk-averse over gains, risk-seeking over losses.

## Criticism

The replication crisis has hit parts of the book. Priming studies are especially shaky. Kahneman himself acknowledged this publicly before his death.

The framework (two systems, cognitive biases generally) still holds. Specific studies should be read with skepticism.

## Why it matters

- Decisions at work: pre-mortems over post-mortems, blind hiring, explicit anchoring awareness
- Personal finance: loss aversion is why selling underperforming stocks feels wrong even when it shouldn't
- Arguments: recognize when you're rationalizing System 1, not reasoning from evidence

Long, repetitive in places, worth the slog.
