# Epistemology

Notes on how we know what we know.

## Types of knowledge

- **A priori** — knowable independent of experience (math, logic)
- **A posteriori** — requires experience (empirical science)
- **Propositional** — knowing THAT something is true ("water is H2O")
- **Procedural** — knowing HOW to do something (riding a bike)
- **Acquaintance** — knowing something directly (knowing a friend vs knowing facts about them)

## Justified true belief

Classical definition: S knows P iff:
1. P is true
2. S believes P
3. S is justified in believing P

## Gettier problems

Cases where all three conditions are met but we hesitate to call it knowledge. Example: you look at a clock that's stopped at 12:00, and happens to be 12:00. You have a justified true belief, but it's lucky.

Various fixes proposed (reliabilism, defeasibility, etc.). No consensus.

## Sources of knowledge

- **Perception** — but perception can deceive
- **Reason** — but starting premises matter
- **Testimony** — most of what we "know" comes from trusting others
- **Memory** — degrades, reconstructs
- **Introspection** — privileged access to our own mental states, but with limits

## Skepticism

- **Global skepticism** — can we know anything? (Descartes' evil demon)
- **Local skepticism** — we know some things, not others
- **Responses** — foundationalism (basic beliefs), coherentism (mutual support), contextualism (standards vary)

## Practical application

- **Calibrate confidence** — distinguish "I'm certain" from "I'm 80% sure"
- **Steelman opposing views** — you don't understand a position until you can argue it
- **Track beliefs over time** — update when evidence warrants, don't just rationalize
- **Distinguish evidence from vibes** — what makes this feel true vs what makes it actually true?
