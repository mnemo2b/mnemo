# Stoicism

Not the modern bro version. The original: Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, Seneca.

## Core distinction

What's in your control:
- Your judgments
- Your intentions
- Your responses

What isn't:
- Events
- Others' actions
- Outcomes

Focus energy on the former. Accept the latter.

## Key ideas

- **Negative visualization** — imagine losing what you have; gratitude returns, attachment loosens
- **View from above** — zoom out on problems; they shrink
- **Dichotomy of control** — the foundation of the entire system
- **Memento mori** — remember you will die; act accordingly
- **Amor fati** — love your fate, not against it but through it

## Key figures

- **Epictetus** — former slave, teacher of the Discourses. Direct, unsparing.
- **Marcus Aurelius** — emperor of Rome, wrote *Meditations* for himself, not publication
- **Seneca** — statesman, playwright, letter-writer. Contradictions noted.
- **Musonius Rufus** — teacher, less famous, practical ethics

## What I use

- **Morning intention** — what will I do today that matters? Where do I expect friction?
- **Evening review** — where did I react rather than respond? What would future-me advise?
- **Preparation** — before hard conversations, picture likely pushback; prepare responses to the person, not the caricature
- **Acceptance** — when something outside my control goes wrong, deliberately drop the narrative "this shouldn't have happened"

## Criticisms

- Can tilt toward passivity if misread
- "Control your response" puts blame on victims of injustice if extended naively
- Sometimes people need to grieve and feel, not reframe

## Not stoicism

- Suppressing emotion (that's closer to apathy or pop misreadings)
- Ignoring suffering — stoics were deeply engaged civically and personally
- Individualism — they viewed duty to others as central
