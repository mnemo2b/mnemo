# Composition

What I look for in the viewfinder.

## Rules that still hold up

- **Rule of thirds** — place subjects on the thirds, not dead center
- **Leading lines** — roads, fences, shorelines draw the eye to the subject
- **Framing** — doorways, branches, shadows wrap the subject
- **Fill the frame** — or leave deliberate negative space

The rules are starting points. Break them when there's a reason.

## Layers

Foreground, middle ground, background. A flat scene becomes dimensional when you include multiple depth layers.

## Simplify

The hardest lesson. Most photos include too much. Step closer, change angle, wait for people to leave the frame. A clean subject on a clean background beats a busy scene 9/10.

## Light

- **Golden hour** (first + last hour of sun) — warm, angled, flattering
- **Blue hour** (dusk, just after sunset) — moody, balanced with city lights
- **Overcast** — soft, flattering for portraits
- **Midday sun** — harsh shadows; shoot in shade or embrace the contrast

## Subject direction

- Eyes should look INTO the frame, not off it
- Motion should have space to move into
- Symmetry works for architecture, less for organic scenes

## Ways to practice

- Shoot the same subject with 10 different compositions
- Review old photos — what would you crop now?
- Study painters, not just photographers (composition heritage comes from painting)
