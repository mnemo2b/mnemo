# Research: Deep Work Claims

## Question

How much of Cal Newport's "Deep Work" thesis holds up under scrutiny? What's the empirical basis?

## Central claims

- Focused attention is increasingly rare and increasingly valuable
- Frequent context-switching degrades cognitive performance significantly
- Skilled knowledge work benefits from sustained, uninterrupted focus
- Shallow work is easily replaceable / automatable

## Supporting evidence

- **Attention residue** (Leroy, 2009) — interruptions cause measurable degradation that persists after returning to task
- **Multitasking costs** (Meyer, Kieras) — well-established; "switching time" is real
- **Expert performance** (Ericsson, deliberate practice) — sustained attention is necessary for skill building
- **Flow state research** (Csikszentmihalyi) — overlap with deep work, but not identical

## Weak points

- "Deep work" as a single category is coarse; different cognitive tasks have different interruption sensitivity
- Field workers, clinicians, teachers, retail — "shallow work" framing dismisses actual skill
- The book cherry-picks examples (Knuth, Carl Jung) who are outliers even in their eras
- "The deep work economy will dominate" is a prediction, not a finding
- Replication issues with some cited studies

## What holds up

- Fragmented attention hurts quality of cognitive work: robust
- Long blocks of focus produce different output than short ones: well-supported
- Modern work environments are adversarial to sustained attention: obvious and documented

## What doesn't

- The specific prescriptions (monastic retreats, grand gestures) aren't empirically compared
- The claim that deep work is "rare and valuable" conflates correlation with causation
- The social / organizational contribution of shallow work is underweighted

## Related research

- **Nass et al.** — heavy multitaskers perform worse on attention tasks
- **Mark et al.** — self-interruption patterns, attention fragmentation
- **Sparrow et al.** — Google effect (storing info externally changes what we retain)
- **Ophir et al.** — media multitaskers have worse working memory filters

## My take

The core idea is right. The self-help packaging oversells specific tactics. Read the research directly if you want the nuance.
