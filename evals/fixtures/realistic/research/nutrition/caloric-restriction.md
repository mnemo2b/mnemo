# Research: Caloric Restriction

## Question

Does caloric restriction (CR) extend human healthspan and/or lifespan?

## Animal data

- **Rodents** — CR robustly extends lifespan, often 20-40%
- **Yeast, worms, flies** — consistent effects across species
- **Monkeys** — mixed. Wisconsin study showed benefit; NIH study didn't. Difference attributed to baseline diet quality of control groups.

## Human data

- **CALERIE trial** (Phase 2) — 2-year 12% calorie reduction in healthy non-obese adults. Improved cardiometabolic markers. Not long enough to measure lifespan.
- **Blue Zones** — correlative, confounded by many factors beyond CR
- **Okinawan longevity data** — historically lower calorie intake, but also diet quality, physical activity, social structure

## Mechanisms

- Reduced insulin signaling
- Autophagy upregulation
- Lower oxidative stress
- Reduced mTOR signaling (cell growth pathway)
- Changes in gene expression (sirtuins, etc.)

## Open questions

- **Quality vs quantity** — is it the calories, the fasting windows, or the food profile?
- **Baseline matters** — CR from overnutrition is different from CR from adequate intake
- **Side effects** — CR causes bone loss, slower wound healing, lower testosterone, irritability
- **Adherence** — humans don't stick to CR long-term

## Related approaches

- **Intermittent fasting** — time-restricted eating; mixed evidence on benefits beyond calorie reduction
- **Protein restriction** — specifically low methionine; animal data strong, human data thin
- **Fasting-mimicking diets** — 5 day low-cal cycles; some clinical benefit, expensive
- **Rapamycin / metformin** — pharmacological mimics of CR; active research area

## My take

The evidence for moderate CR (maintaining healthy weight, avoiding chronic overnutrition) is strong for healthspan. The evidence for aggressive CR is weaker and the tradeoffs are real. Easier to get 80% of the benefit via body composition and quality diet than severe restriction.
