# Research: Protein Intake Studies

## Question

What does the current evidence actually support for daily protein intake in trained vs untrained adults?

## RDA vs practice

- **RDA: 0.8 g/kg bodyweight** — set for minimum to prevent deficiency, not optimization
- **Endurance athletes: 1.2-1.4 g/kg**
- **Resistance-trained: 1.6-2.2 g/kg** — meta-analyses converge here
- **Older adults: higher end** — anabolic resistance reduces response per gram
- **Weight loss: upper end + higher** — preserve lean mass in a deficit

## Meta-analyses

- **Morton et al. (2018)** — systematic review, no benefit beyond ~1.6 g/kg for hypertrophy
- **Bandegan et al.** — IAAO (indicator amino acid oxidation) method suggests up to 2.2 g/kg for older active adults
- **Phillips et al.** — pro-per-meal thresholds of 0.4-0.5 g/kg, 4 meals a day

## Timing

- Less dogma than 10 years ago
- 20-40g per meal, 3-5 meals/day is enough for muscle protein synthesis
- "Anabolic window" post-workout exaggerated in lay fitness culture
- Protein before bed (casein) has modest but real overnight synthesis benefit

## Sources

- Plant vs animal protein: animal is slightly more effective per gram (leucine content, digestibility) but plant works if total intake is higher
- Whey is faster-digesting than casein; doesn't matter much long-term
- Whole food vs supplement: supplement is just convenience

## Open questions

- How does the recommendation scale for very high training volumes?
- Does protein intake affect longevity? (mTOR signaling concerns overstated.)
- What's the real ceiling? Evidence above 2.2 g/kg is thin.
