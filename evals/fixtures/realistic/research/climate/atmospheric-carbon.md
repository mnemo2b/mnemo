# Research: Atmospheric Carbon

## Question

Where are we actually on atmospheric CO2, what's the trajectory, and what interventions are plausibly effective?

## Current state

- CO2 concentration in 2024-2025: ~425 ppm (Mauna Loa, monthly averages)
- Pre-industrial: ~280 ppm
- Annual rise: ~2.5-3 ppm/year
- Current atmospheric CO2 is highest in ~3 million years (paleoclimate proxies)

## Trajectory

- Emissions still growing globally, though per-capita flat/declining in OECD
- China + India + rest of developing world drives growth
- Energy transition accelerating (solar, wind, batteries) but not fast enough to reverse trend

## Effective interventions (evidence-ranked)

**Strong evidence for impact:**
- Electrification of grid + transport
- Renewable energy expansion (solar now cheapest form in most markets)
- Reducing deforestation
- Energy efficiency in buildings

**Moderate evidence:**
- Nuclear (if politically viable)
- Carbon capture and storage at point sources (cement, steel)
- Methane leak reduction (quick wins, high GWP)

**Speculative or niche:**
- Direct Air Capture (DAC) — expensive, not yet scaled
- Geoengineering (SAI) — risky, untested at scale, governance nightmare
- Ocean iron fertilization — inconclusive, possible ecological harm

## What won't help

- Individual carbon offsets from forestry projects (large fraction are junk)
- "Clean coal" (wasn't, isn't)
- Hydrogen for consumer energy (storage losses make it round-trip inefficient)
- Voluntary pledges without enforcement

## Key sources

- IPCC AR6 reports
- IEA World Energy Outlook (annual)
- NOAA Global Monitoring Laboratory
- Our World in Data energy section
- Hannah Ritchie's work

## Open questions

- What's the realistic ceiling? 2°C looks out of reach without overshoot
- How much of the energy transition is tech-constrained vs policy-constrained?
- What's the cost-benefit of adaptation vs mitigation investment going forward?
