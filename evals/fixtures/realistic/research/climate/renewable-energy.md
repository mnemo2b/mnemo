# Research: Renewable Energy

## Question

Where are we on renewable deployment, what are the bottlenecks, and what's the realistic near-term shape of the grid?

## Deployment trends

- **Solar** — now cheapest form of electricity in most sunny regions. Global installed capacity doubling every 2-3 years.
- **Wind** — mature onshore, growing offshore. Capacity factors continue to improve as turbines scale.
- **Batteries** — price per kWh dropping ~15% annually. Grid-scale deployment ramping hard.
- **Geothermal** — enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) show promise; Fervo and others scaling
- **Nuclear** — flat in most regions, growing in China, restarting in Japan. SMRs still pre-commercial at scale.

## Bottlenecks

- **Grid interconnection** — massive backlog in the US. Projects wait 3-5+ years.
- **Transmission** — lines to move power from windy/sunny areas to load centers
- **Permitting** — local opposition, siting challenges
- **Skilled labor** — electricians, linemen, solar installers all constrained
- **Critical minerals** — lithium, cobalt, nickel supply chains concentrated in a few countries
- **Rate design** — utility incentives not always aligned with decarbonization

## Grid realities

- Intermittency is manageable up to ~50-70% renewable penetration with current tools
- Beyond that, you need: long-duration storage, HVDC transmission, demand response, or firm backup (nuclear, gas with CCS, geothermal)
- "Baseload" vs "variable" is less useful framing than "firm vs variable" — nuclear is firm but inflexible; demand response is variable and fast

## Storage

- **Lithium-ion** — 4-hr duration economical; 8+ hr getting there
- **Iron-air** (Form Energy) — promising for 100-hour durations
- **Pumped hydro** — oldest grid storage; siting-constrained
- **Thermal storage** — niche, but relevant for industrial heat

## What's under-discussed

- Transmission is the hardest problem
- Electrification of end uses (cars, heat pumps) reshapes demand curves entirely
- Heat is 50%+ of energy use; electrifying is much harder than electrifying light-duty vehicles
- Industrial decarbonization (steel, cement) needs different tools than grid decarb
